FAQs about Jubilee's Volunteer Program

Q

Do I have to be Christian to be a volunteer?

Jubilee seeks to understand and live out discipleship to Jesus Christ. We do not require volunteers to claim a particular creed or faith statememnt, but we hope each person will explore discipleship through the worship and devotional life of the community. We hope that our life together provides challenge, support, and imagination for how individuals might continue to grow in spiritual faith and practices after leaving Jubilee.

Q

What is the average age of volunteers?

The majority of volunteers are in their twenties, usually in a post-college stage of life. Other volunteers may be middle-aged or retired. The summer term usually has the largest number of volunteers still in college. Most volunteers are single, but a number are married. Families can be accepted into the volunteer program only if suitable housing space is available at that time.

 

Q

What if I can’t arrive at the start of the term, or if I have to leave before the term ends?

We need volunteers to be here for the whole term, and therefore ask each volunteer to commit to being here for the entire term. The first week of each term is filled solid with orientation and training for teaching the ESOL classes. It is essential to be here for that, so we do not invite volunteers who cannot arrive on time. The ending date has only slightly more flexibility than the starting date.

Q

What if I would need to be away during the term?

We expect volunteers to be here for the whole term. There are emergencies that arise, such as illness or death in the family, etc. Time away can be negotiated when these things happen. Any non-emergency requests (such as being in a family member’s wedding), should be discussed with the volunteer coordinator at the time one applies. We cannot approve every kind of request. (For example, we don’t approve taking a long weekend to go visit friends in other places.) We see being a volunteer as a full-time commitment.

Q

Are weekends free? What happens on weekends?

We work about 40 hours per week, which includes 2 ½ hours on Saturday mornings. We work on Saturday mornings so that we can provide an English class to the refugees that day. From Saturday afternoon until Sunday worship, the schedule is usually free.  Volunteers have personal time, hang out, and play games or do activities with the refugees. (Soccer, frisbee, and volleyball are big favorites.) Sometimes there is a group trip to Athens, Georgia. There is no public transportation that comes to Comer, and Athens is about 30 minutes distant by car, so volunteer trips to Athens happen only when a group is going.

Q

Can family or friends come visit me at Jubilee?

Yes. We have guest rooms available most of the time. Guests are limited to one-week stays, and we encourage them to join in our work projects. The volunteer is expected to continue with usual work assignments while their visitors are here unless other arrangements are made with the Volunteer Coordinator.

Q

Can I stay for more than one term?

Extensions for a second (or even third) term are granted provided that it is mutually positive for the individual and for the community. There is a process to evaluate that.

Q

What kinds of work will I be expected to do?

Volunteers do a mix of things: teaching English language classes and doing refugee childcare usually take up at least half the work week. Other typical work duties are gardening, food processing, animal chores, cleaning refugee houses, office work, and light maintenance, such as painting or grounds upkeep. We welcome volunteers with special skills, such as construction, auto mechanics, maintenance skills, or computer upkeep. We do provide training for teaching ESOL; no previous experience is required.

Q

If I have a specific interest in one work area, can I arrange my schedule around it?

All volunteers do a mix of jobs while they are at Jubilee. We especially encourage each volunteer to do some teaching of ESOL classes and help with refugee childcare, as these are the main points of connection with the refugees. The amount of work hours spent doing these jobs varies with the number of refugees and the number of volunteers. Some volunteers come with a primary interest in organic gardening, or milking the cow and making butter or cheese. When possible, we will schedule your time in areas of your greatest interest, but we can't guarantee anything. It’s important for us that volunteers come with a willingness to do whatever needs to be done in the community. Our highest work priority is meeting the needs of refugees.

Q

Can I bring my car?

Yes. Some volunteers arrive by public transportation, but you are welcome to bring your own car.

Q

What are the room accommodations like?

Most rooms are double occupancy rooms along an upstairs hall with a shared bathroom. Most rooms have sleeping lofts.  No staff housing has air conditioning.

Q

How is being a volunteer at Jubilee different from other volunteer settings?

Being a volunteer at Jubilee is meant to be an holistic experience - here you don't finish your workday and then leave and go somewhere else that you consider home. At Jubilee the work and non-work parts of the day blend together into a rich (and full) experience. Here you work, play, eat, and worship with many of the same people - that's what living in intentional community means for us. So at 5:30pm when you're done with work, there are community meals to be prepared and cleaned up after and there are often activities or meetings in the evenings. We often enjoy making music or doing crafts together as a way of building relationships. You might visit in the evenings with some of the refugees who are your students in classes during the day. Life in community can be intense, but is usually deeply rewarding.
